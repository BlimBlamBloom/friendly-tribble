Exploring Shanghai’s Hidden Gems: Seven Lesser-Known Places to Visit

Shanghai is a city known for its towering skyscrapers, bustling markets, and vibrant culture. While popular tourist attractions like The Bund and Yu Garden are must-see spots, there are also many lesser-known places that offer a unique glimpse into the city’s rich history and culture. Here are seven hidden gems in Shanghai that are worth exploring.

1. Jing'an Sculpture Park

Located in the heart of Shanghai, Jing'an Sculpture Park is a peaceful urban oasis that offers a blend of nature and art. The park is home to a wide array of contemporary sculptures and art installations, making it an ideal spot for a leisurely stroll. The modern sculptures contrast beautifully with the park's lush greenery, creating a serene atmosphere where visitors can relax and enjoy the artistic displays. Whether you’re an art enthusiast or just looking for a quiet escape from the city’s hustle and bustle, Jing'an Sculpture Park is a place worth visiting.

2. Shikumen Open House Museum

Nestled in the trendy Xintiandi area, the Shikumen Open House Museum offers a fascinating glimpse into traditional Shanghainese life. The museum is set in a classic Shikumen-style house, which is a unique architectural style combining Western and Chinese elements. Inside, the museum is furnished with period furniture and artefacts, recreating the atmosphere of a typical Shanghai home in the early 20th century. This museum not only preserves a piece of Shanghai’s architectural heritage but also provides insight into the daily lives of its residents during that era.

3. Lu Xun Park

Lu Xun Park, named after one of China’s most famous writers, is a tranquil green space in the Hongkou District. The park is a popular spot for locals who come to exercise, fly kites, and enjoy leisurely walks. The park also houses a memorial hall and the tomb of Lu Xun, where visitors can learn about his life and contributions to Chinese literature. The combination of natural beauty and cultural significance makes Lu Xun Park a meaningful place to visit in Shanghai.

4. Former Residence of Sun Yat-sen

The Former Residence of Sun Yat-sen is a beautifully preserved historical site located in Shanghai’s French Concession area. Sun Yat-sen, often referred to as the "Father of Modern China," lived here with his wife, Soong Ching-ling, from 1918 to 1924. The residence offers visitors a glimpse into the daily life of one of China’s most influential figures. This well-preserved home is a quiet retreat in the bustling city, allowing visitors to reflect on the history and legacy of Sun Yat-sen.

5. Qibao Ancient Town

For those interested in traditional Chinese culture, Qibao Ancient Town is a must-visit destination. Located in the Minhang District, Qibao is a well-preserved water town that dates back over a thousand years. The town’s narrow lanes are lined with traditional architecture, shops selling local crafts, and street food vendors offering delicious snacks. The canals running through the town add to its charm, making it a picturesque place to explore. Visitors can also visit the Qibao Temple and enjoy boat rides along the waterway.

6. Wukang Mansion

Wukang Mansion, also known as the Normandie Apartments, is an iconic building in Shanghai’s French Concession. Designed by Hungarian architect László Hudec in 1924, the building is known for its elegant, curved façade and European architectural style. Wukang Mansion has housed many famous residents over the years, including Chinese celebrities and intellectuals. Today, it remains a symbol of the city’s rich history and architectural beauty. The building is surrounded by charming streets filled with cafes, boutiques, and galleries, making it a perfect spot for a leisurely walk.

7. Shanghai Film Museum

For cinema enthusiasts, the Shanghai Film Museum is a treasure trove of Chinese film history. Located in the former Shanghai Film Studio, the museum covers over 100 years of filmmaking in China. Exhibits include vintage film equipment, posters, and costumes, as well as interactive displays that allow visitors to experience the filmmaking process. The museum celebrates the contributions of Shanghai to the global film industry and offers a fascinating look at the evolution of Chinese cinema.
